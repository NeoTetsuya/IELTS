What is Parallel Structure?

Parallel structure means using the same pattern of words to show that two or more ideas have the same level of importance. This happens in lists or series. When you keep the structure parallel, your writing is smoother and easier to read.

üí° The "Matching Outfits" Analogy

Think of the items in your list as people getting dressed for an event. If the first person wears a verb ending in "-ing", everyone else in the list should too. They all need matching grammatical outfits!

Why it was wrong:

The original sentence mixed different verb forms: two "-ing" words ('hiking', 'swimming') and one infinitive ('to ride').

At the Phrase Level

‚ùå Not Parallel:

‚úÖ Parallel:

The teacher said he was a poor student because he waited until the last minute, completed his lab problems in a careless manner, and lacked motivation.

Why it was wrong:

The original sentence mixed two verb phrases ('waited...', 'completed...') with a clause ('his motivation was low').

At the Clause Level

‚ùå Not Parallel:

‚úÖ Parallel:

The coach told the players that they should get a lot of sleep, that they should not eat too much, and that they should do some warm-up exercises.

Why it was wrong:

The original sentence mixed two "that" clauses with an infinitive phrase ('to do...').

How to Check for Parallelism

A quick way to check your work is to scan your paper for the coordinating conjunctions: and, but, or. When you find one, pause and look at the grammatical structure on each side of it. Do they match? If not, you've found a spot to fix!
